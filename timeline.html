Sustainability Milestones Across Motorsport

From the first hybrid boost to 100% renewable fuels – a timeline of green progress in every major racing series.

2009 Formula 1

F1 introduces the Kinetic Energy Recovery System (KERS) – the first hybrid system in top‑level motorsport.

KERS gave drivers an extra 80 hp for 6.7 seconds per lap, recovering energy under braking. It proved that hybrid technology could survive the harsh environment of racing, setting the stage for the turbo‑hybrid era. Teams like McLaren and Ferrari developed the system, and its principles later trickled down into road car regenerative braking systems.

2012 WEC

Toyota TS030 Hybrid debuts in WEC, using supercapacitor‑based hybrid system – wins three races.

The TS030 used a unique front‑axle electric motor for all‑wheel‑drive hybrid boost. Its supercapacitor could charge and discharge rapidly, inspiring future road car hybrids like the Toyota RAV4 Prime. This was the first race‑winning hybrid LMP1 car, proving hybrid endurance could beat traditional diesels.

2014 Formula 1 & Formula E

F1 adopts 1.6L V6 turbo‑hybrid engines; Formula E debuts as the world’s first all‑electric single‑seater championship.

F1’s new power units cut fuel consumption by 30% overnight while maintaining similar lap times. The MGU‑H pioneered exhaust energy recovery. Meanwhile, Formula E started racing on temporary street circuits, bringing zero‑emission mobility to city centres and proving that electric racing could be exciting and commercially viable.

2015–2017 WEC

Porsche 919 Hybrid achieves 900+ hp using a 2.0L V4 turbo, two energy recovery systems, and sets efficiency records.

The 919’s complex hybrid system recovered energy from the exhaust (like F1’s MGU‑H) and the front axle under braking. It could complete a lap of Le Mans using 30% less fuel than previous prototypes. Its 8 MJ hybrid system technology later influenced Porsche’s Taycan EV and 918 Spyder hypercar.

2018 Formula E, F1

Formula E Gen2 car eliminates mid‑race car swaps; Red Bull Racing becomes first F1 team to achieve carbon neutral status.

The Gen2’s 54 kWh battery doubled the range, allowing a full race distance. Red Bull’s carbon neutrality, achieved through renewable energy at its factory and offsetting, set a precedent that other teams followed. This year also saw F1 announce its Net Zero 2030 ambition.

2019 Extreme E, WEC

Extreme E is launched – electric SUV racing in climate‑affected locations; WEC announces Hypercar class with hybrid & renewable fuel.

Extreme E’s mission combines sport with activism, using hydrogen fuel cells for remote power. WEC’s Hypercar rules allow manufacturers to race with hybrid or non‑hybrid powertrains on biofuels, encouraging diversity in sustainable solutions.

2022 WRC, F1

WRC introduces Rally1 plug‑in hybrid cars; Formula 1 adopts E10 fuel (10% ethanol) and commits to Net Zero by 2030.

Rally1 cars use a 100 kW electric motor and a 3.9 kWh battery, with drivers controlling the boost. F1’s E10 fuel is a stepping stone to 100% sustainable fuel in 2026. Several teams, including Mercedes and McLaren, achieve carbon neutral certification.

2023 IndyCar, Formula E

IndyCar switches to Shell 100% renewable race fuel; Formula E Gen3 car debuts with 600 kW and no rear brakes.

IndyCar’s fuel, made from sugarcane waste, reduces lifecycle greenhouse gases by 60%. The Gen3 car recovers 40% of its energy via regenerative braking and is the most efficient racing machine ever, with 90%+ motor efficiency.

2024 MotoGP, IndyCar

MotoGP mandates at least 40% non‑fossil fuel; IndyCar introduces hybrid system with supercapacitor.

MotoGP’s fuel is a stepping stone to 100% sustainable by 2027. IndyCar’s hybrid unit provides a push‑to‑pass boost similar to KERS, improving overtaking and efficiency, and is being co‑developed with Honda and Chevrolet for potential road car use.

2025 WRC

World Rally Championship becomes the first FIA World Championship to run on 100% sustainable fuel.

This fuel, developed by P1 Fuels, is made from non‑food biomass and can be used in any petrol car without modification. WRC’s commitment will save over 100,000 tonnes of CO₂ annually and provide a blueprint for other series.

2026 Formula 1

F1 introduces next‑gen power units: 50% electric, 50% combustion, running on 100% carbon‑neutral e‑fuels.

The new engines remove the complex MGU‑H, increase electric power to 350 kW, and use advanced sustainable fuel. This shift is expected to attract new manufacturers (Audi, Ford) and accelerate the global supply of synthetic fuels for aviation and shipping.

2027 WEC / Le Mans, MotoGP

Le Mans launches hydrogen‑powered class; MotoGP achieves 100% sustainable fuel.

Hydrogen prototypes will race at the 24 Hours of Le Mans, using either fuel cells or hydrogen combustion engines. MotoGP’s switch to 100% non‑fossil fuel will make it the first two‑wheel world championship to run entirely on sustainable energy.

2030 Multiple Series

Formula 1 achieves Net Zero across operations, logistics, and events. WEC, IndyCar, and others target similar goals.

By 2030, every F1 event will be powered by renewable energy, all freight will use biofuel or electric transport, and fan travel emissions will be offset. Other series, including WEC and MotoGP, aim for carbon neutrality in the same timeframe, creating a global network of sustainable sporting events.

2040 Vision

Fully circular motorsport: zero‑waste cars, tracks as power plants, and carbon‑negative events.

Race cars will be built from 100% recycled or bio‑based materials, with components composted or remanufactured at end of life. Circuits will produce more energy than they consume, and the entire motorsport ecosystem will be a net‑positive contributor to the environment, inspiring other industries to follow.
