Battery Technology Born on Track

Racing demands batteries that are light, powerful, and capable of charging/discharging hundreds of times a race – a brutal testing ground that benefits every EV on the road.

High Power vs. High Energy

Road cars need range, so their batteries are optimised for energy density – how much energy they can store per kilogram. Racing cars, however, prioritise power density: how quickly they can charge and discharge. In a 90‑minute Formula 1 race, the battery cycles between full and empty hundreds of times, enduring extreme temperatures and vibrations. The current F1 battery weighs just 20 kg and can deliver 120 kW instantly. Formula E’s Gen3 battery pushes this even further: 600 kW of total power from a 285 kg pack, with the ability to receive a 600 hp charge in just 30 seconds during a pit stop.

Thermal Management – The Hidden Hero

Overheating is the enemy of battery longevity and safety. Racing has pioneered liquid cooling systems that can keep cells at optimal temperatures even under massive current flows. The algorithms that control this cooling – deciding exactly when to chill or warm the battery – were developed for racing and are now embedded in the Battery Management Systems (BMS) of every major EV brand. Tesla, for instance, uses predictive thermal conditioning learned from racing to prepare its batteries for fast charging.

Second Life and Recycling

What happens to a race battery when it can no longer deliver the peak power needed on track? It gets a second life. Formula E batteries are repurposed for grid storage and solar micro‑grids in remote communities. The series’ partnership with Umicore ensures that every battery cell is eventually recycled, recovering valuable lithium, cobalt, and nickel. This circular economy approach is a blueprint for the global EV industry, which faces a tsunami of used batteries in the coming decades.

Supercapacitors – An Alternative Path

Some series are choosing supercapacitors over batteries. IndyCar’s 2024 hybrid system uses a supercapacitor because it charges and discharges in a fraction of a second – perfect for push‑to‑pass overtaking. Toyota’s early WEC hybrid also used supercapacitors. These devices don’t store as much total energy, but their instantaneous response and almost unlimited cycle life offer lessons for future mild‑hybrid road cars.

Consumer Electronics Benefit Too

The rapid cycling tests that racing batteries endure have directly improved the lifespan of smartphone and laptop batteries. Techniques for preventing dendrite growth (which causes short circuits) and for managing charge at extreme temperatures were pioneered in motorsport R&D labs. Even the fast‑charging protocols we use for our phones owe a debt to racing’s need to shove huge amounts of energy into a battery in seconds.
