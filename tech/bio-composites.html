Bio‑Composites & Natural Fibres

Not everything on a race car needs to be carbon fibre. Flax, hemp, and even banana fibres are finding their way onto the track – making cars lighter, safer, and carbon‑negative.

Why Natural Fibres?

Traditional carbon fibre is made from polyacrylonitrile, a petroleum derivative. Its production is energy‑intensive and generates significant CO₂. Natural fibres like flax, hemp, jute, and kenaf absorb CO₂ as they grow, making them carbon‑negative at the raw material stage. When combined with bio‑based resins, they can form a fully compostable composite – a material that returns to the earth at the end of its life.

Racing Applications

McLaren was one of the first to use a flax‑fibre composite in a racing context – the seat of the McLaren 720S road car, later adapted for GT3 racing. The material offers excellent vibration damping and is safer in a crash because it doesn’t shatter into sharp splinters like carbon fibre. Formula E’s Gen3 car uses natural‑fibre body panels for non‑structural parts. Extreme E mandates that all teams use flax‑fibre bodywork, proving it can survive the harshest off‑road conditions.

The Performance Advantage

Bio‑composites are not as stiff as carbon fibre, but that can be an advantage. Their natural flexibility provides better impact absorption, which is why they are used in crash structures and suspension components. They are also lighter – flax fibre has a density of 1.5 g/cm³ compared to carbon fibre’s 1.8 g/cm³ – so a switch can save weight even before considering the environmental benefit.

Beyond the Track

The auto industry is watching closely. BMW i3 uses kenaf fibres in its door panels. The furniture and architecture industries are adopting flax composite for chairs and building facades. Motorsport is the high‑profile testbed that proves these materials can perform at the highest level, which encourages wider adoption.
