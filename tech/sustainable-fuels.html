100% Sustainable Fuels – The End of Fossil Oil on Track

How motorsport is ditching fossil oil without sacrificing speed – and kick‑starting a global clean‑fuel industry.

The 2026 Revolution and Beyond

From 2026, every Formula 1 car will run on fully synthetic, carbon‑neutral fuel. It is chemically identical to petrol but made without extracting a single drop of crude oil. The fuel is “drop‑in” – meaning it works in any existing petrol engine with no modifications whatsoever. This isn’t a distant dream; Porsche, Audi, and other manufacturers have already proven the concept on the road and in the 24 Hours of Le Mans.

But F1 is far from the only series making the switch. The World Rally Championship (WRC) has committed to 100% sustainable fuel from 2025, making it the first FIA World Championship to run entirely on non‑fossil fuel. The World Endurance Championship (WEC) allows renewable fuels in all classes, and IndyCar switched to a 100% renewable race fuel in 2023. MotoGP is on a path to 100% sustainable fuel by 2027.

How Are Sustainable Fuels Made?

There are two main pathways, both of which close the carbon cycle:

1. E‑fuels (Synthetic Fuels)

E‑fuels are created by combining green hydrogen (produced by splitting water using renewable electricity – solar or wind) with carbon dioxide captured directly from the atmosphere or from industrial processes. The result is a liquid hydrocarbon that can be refined into petrol, diesel, or jet fuel. Porsche’s Haru Oni pilot plant in Chile uses wind power to produce e‑fuel and plans to scale to 550 million litres per year. F1’s 2026 fuel supplier, Aramco, is investing heavily in similar technology.

Because the CO₂ released when the fuel burns was originally captured from the air, the process is net zero. No new carbon enters the atmosphere.

2. Advanced Biofuels

Second‑generation biofuels are made from non‑food biomass: agricultural waste (corn stover, rice husks), forestry residues, or even municipal solid waste. The feedstock is gasified into synthesis gas (a mixture of carbon monoxide and hydrogen) and then converted into liquid fuel via Fischer‑Tropsch synthesis. Companies like P1 Fuels (WRC supplier) and Shell (IndyCar supplier) are leading this field. These fuels reduce lifecycle greenhouse gas emissions by over 80% compared to fossil petrol.

Why Not Just Go Electric?

Electrification is essential, but it is not the whole answer. Heavy‑duty transport, shipping, aviation, and even classic cars rely on liquid fuels’ immense energy density. A battery cannot yet match the 12 kWh of energy contained in a single kilogram of petrol. Sustainable fuels provide a way to decarbonise those hard‑to‑electrify sectors immediately, using the same engines and infrastructure we already have. Motorsport is acting as the catalyst, creating demand that drives down costs and builds a global supply chain.

Series‑by‑Series Adoption

  • Formula 1: 100% sustainable drop‑in fuel from 2026.
  • WRC: 100% sustainable fuel from 2025 (P1 Fuels).
  • WEC / Le Mans: Renewable fuels allowed in Hypercar and LMDh; 100% for all competitors by 2025.
  • IndyCar: 100% renewable fuel (Shell) from 2023.
  • MotoGP: 40% non‑fossil from 2024, 100% by 2027.
  • Extreme E: Uses hydrogen fuel cells for charging, effectively running on green hydrogen.

Real‑World Impact

The motorsport‑driven push for sustainable fuels has already had ripple effects. Lufthansa and United Airlines have signed offtake agreements for synthetic aviation fuel. Maersk is exploring e‑methanol for cargo ships. Porsche’s e‑fuel plant will supply not only race cars but also the brand’s road cars and classic vehicle programs. Every litre of sustainable fuel burned on a racetrack is a litre of fossil fuel left in the ground – and a step toward a global, carbon‑neutral fuel market.
