Energy Recovery System (ERS)

The technology that turned racing cars into moving power plants – capturing energy that used to go to waste.

What is Energy Recovery?

Every time a race car brakes, kinetic energy is turned into heat and lost. Similarly, the exhaust gases carry enormous thermal energy that simply blows away. An Energy Recovery System (ERS) captures both of these energy streams and converts them into electricity, which is stored in a battery. That electricity can then be deployed to provide a powerful acceleration boost – or to reduce the amount of fuel the engine must burn.

In Formula 1, the current hybrid V6 turbo engine actually consists of two separate recovery units: the MGU‑K (Motor Generator Unit – Kinetic) and the MGU‑H (Motor Generator Unit – Heat). Together they can harvest up to 4 megajoules of energy per lap and redeploy it as 160 extra horsepower. That’s enough to shave seconds off lap times while using 30% less fuel than pre‑2014 engines.

How the MGU‑K Works

The MGU‑K is connected to the crankshaft and acts as both a generator and a motor. When the driver lifts off the throttle and applies the brakes, the MGU‑K switches to generator mode, using the car’s momentum to spin an electric motor. This slows the car down (just like regenerative braking in a Toyota Prius or Tesla) and charges the battery. Under acceleration, the MGU‑K reverses its role and delivers up to 120 kW (160 hp) of extra power to the rear wheels for around 33 seconds per lap.

How the MGU‑H Works

The MGU‑H sits between the turbine and compressor of the turbocharger. Hot exhaust gases spin the turbine, but instead of letting excess energy go, the MGU‑H harvests it as electricity. It can also act as a motor, spinning the turbo up from low speeds to eliminate turbo lag. This not only improves throttle response but also enables the engine to run in a very lean, efficient combustion mode. The MGU‑H can generate unlimited energy per lap (unlike the MGU‑K’s 2 MJ limit) and is a critical part of the 50% thermal efficiency achievement.

Real‑World Impact

Regenerative braking is now standard on almost every hybrid and electric road car. The rapid charge/discharge cycles tested in F1 batteries have improved lithium‑ion battery longevity for consumer electronics and grid storage. The learnings from the MGU‑H have influenced the design of electric‑assist turbochargers now found in high‑efficiency road engines from Mercedes, Audi, and others.

In endurance racing (WEC), the LMDh prototypes use a spec hybrid system with a rear‑axle MGU‑K, proving that energy recovery is not just for sprint races. It’s a fundamental building block of a sustainable future.
