Energy Recovery System (ERS) – Wasting Nothing

The technology that turned racing cars into moving power plants – capturing energy that used to be lost as heat and noise.

What Is Energy Recovery?

Every time a car brakes, kinetic energy is transformed into heat by the friction between brake pads and discs. In a conventional car that heat is simply wasted. An Energy Recovery System (ERS) captures a large portion of that energy, converts it into electricity, stores it in a battery, and then releases it to provide an acceleration boost – or to reduce the amount of fuel the engine must burn.

In top‑level motorsport today, ERS is not a single device but a complete hybrid architecture. In Formula 1 it consists of two Motor Generator Units: the MGU‑K (Kinetic) and the MGU‑H (Heat). Together they can harvest up to 4 MJ of energy per lap and redeploy it as an extra 160 horsepower for around 33 seconds per lap. That is enough to shave off seconds while using 30% less fuel than pre‑2014 engines.

The MGU‑K – Kinetic Energy Recovery

The MGU‑K is connected directly to the crankshaft and acts as both an electric motor and a generator. Under braking, the system switches to generator mode: the rear wheels spin the MGU‑K, which produces electricity and slows the car down – just like the regenerative braking in a Toyota Prius or a Tesla. This electricity is stored in a high‑voltage lithium‑ion battery. Under acceleration, the MGU‑K reverses its role and delivers up to 120 kW (160 hp) of extra power to the rear wheels. The driver can deploy this boost manually, or the engine control unit can do it automatically for maximum lap time.

The amount of energy that can be recovered by the MGU‑K is limited by the regulations to 2 MJ per lap, but the system is so efficient that it actually recovers far more – the excess is simply not stored. The technology has forced rapid improvements in battery power density, charging speed, and thermal management, all of which now benefit road cars.

The MGU‑H – Heat Energy Recovery

The MGU‑H is unique to Formula 1’s current power units (and was used in some LMP1 prototypes like the Porsche 919 Hybrid). It sits between the turbine and compressor of the turbocharger. Exhaust gases spin the turbine; the MGU‑H harvests the surplus rotational energy and turns it into electricity. It can also function as a motor, spinning the turbo up to 100,000 rpm almost instantly to eliminate turbo lag. This allows the engine to run a very lean air‑fuel mixture – burning less fuel while producing the same power – and contributes massively to the engine’s incredible 50% thermal efficiency.

The MGU‑H can recover an unlimited amount of energy per lap (unlike the MGU‑K’s 2 MJ cap). This energy can either be sent to the battery or directly to the MGU‑K for immediate deployment, bypassing the battery entirely. This “direct drive” reduces losses and further improves efficiency.

Beyond Formula 1 – Energy Recovery in Other Series

  • WEC / Le Mans (LMDh): The current LMDh rules use a spec hybrid system on the rear axle, delivering a 50 hp electric boost. The system is simpler than F1’s but equally effective at recovering braking energy.
  • WRC Rally1: Since 2022, top‑level rally cars use a plug‑in hybrid system with a 100 kW electric motor and a 3.9 kWh battery. The hybrid boost is driver‑activated and recharges under braking.
  • IndyCar (2024): The new hybrid unit pairs an MGU with a supercapacitor, providing a push‑to‑pass overtaking boost while recovering energy under braking – a direct descendant of F1’s KERS.
  • Formula E: While not a hybrid, Formula E’s Gen3 car uses regenerative braking so aggressively that it has no rear hydraulic brakes – all deceleration is done by the electric motors, recovering energy and sending it back to the battery.

Real‑World Trickle‑Down

Regenerative braking is now standard on virtually every hybrid and electric road car. The rapid charge/discharge cycles tested in racing batteries have improved the longevity of lithium‑ion cells in consumer electronics and grid storage. The cooling and battery management algorithms developed by F1 teams have been licensed to manufacturers of electric buses and trucks. Even the concept of “one‑pedal driving” in EVs (where lifting off the accelerator causes strong regenerative braking) was refined using knowledge gained on the track.
