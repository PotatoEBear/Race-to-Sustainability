Hydrogen – The Next Frontier

Hydrogen can be burned in a combustion engine or used in a fuel cell. Both paths are being explored in top‑level motorsport – and one of them could power your future car.

Hydrogen Combustion – The Sound of Sustainability

Toyota, a pioneer in hydrogen, has been testing a hydrogen‑fuelled internal combustion engine in its GR Corolla race car since 2021. The engine sounds exactly like a petrol engine, revs to high RPM, and emits only water vapour. The technology is now being adopted by other manufacturers and will form the basis of a new hydrogen class at the 24 Hours of Le Mans from 2027. The class will allow teams to choose between hydrogen combustion and hydrogen fuel cells, creating a direct head‑to‑head test of the two most promising zero‑emission paths.

Fuel Cells – Silent, Efficient Power

Unlike combustion, a fuel cell converts hydrogen directly into electricity through a chemical reaction with oxygen, with water as the only by‑product. Fuel cell vehicles (FCEVs) are already on the road – the Toyota Mirai, Hyundai Nexo – but racing pushes the technology to its limits. Extreme E uses a mobile hydrogen fuel cell generator to charge its electric race cars in remote locations, demonstrating that hydrogen can be a practical, off‑grid energy source. This same principle could power construction sites, disaster relief, or temporary events.

The Green Hydrogen Challenge

Not all hydrogen is clean. Grey hydrogen is made from natural gas, releasing CO₂. Motorsport is driving demand for green hydrogen – produced by electrolysis using renewable energy. The refuelling infrastructure for a race event is a miniature version of what a future hydrogen society would need. By solving the logistics of transporting and storing hydrogen safely in a high‑pressure race environment, motorsport is paving the way for its use in trucking, shipping, and even aviation.

Why Hydrogen Complements Batteries

Battery‑electric vehicles are excellent for short trips, but they struggle with very long range and heavy payloads. Hydrogen offers a high energy‑density alternative that can be refuelled in minutes. Racing validates both technologies and shows that the future is not about choosing one – it’s about using the right tool for the job. A Formula E car is perfect for a city street circuit; a hydrogen Le Mans prototype could cover 5,000 km in 24 hours with zero emissions.

Road‑Car Spin‑offs

Toyota’s hydrogen‑combustion engine development is directly linked to its racing program. BMW is also testing hydrogen‑powered road cars using knowledge from motorsport. The fuel cell stacks being refined for Le Mans prototypes will find their way into heavy‑duty trucks and buses. Racing proves that hydrogen can be exciting, safe, and practical – and that makes it easier for governments and industry to invest in the necessary infrastructure.
